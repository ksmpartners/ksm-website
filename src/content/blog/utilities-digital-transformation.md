Utility digital transformation has been a buzzword for a decade, but the results have been uneven. Some utilities have fundamentally changed how they operate — improving grid reliability, reducing costs, and building new capabilities. Others have spent significant capital on technology programs that underdelivered.

After working with utilities on transformation programs of all sizes, we've observed some consistent patterns that distinguish the programs that succeed.

## 1. OT/IT Integration Is the Hard Part

Most digital transformation conversations focus on enterprise IT — cloud migrations, analytics platforms, modern applications. But in a utility, the most operationally significant data lives in the operational technology (OT) environment: SCADA systems, protection relays, historian databases, and field devices.

Getting data out of OT safely — without compromising security isolation or operational reliability — is technically challenging. Utilities that treat this as a straightforward integration problem consistently underestimate the effort. Those that staff OT/IT integration expertise early fare significantly better.

## 2. The Data Foundation Has to Come First

Analytics, AI, and advanced operations all require trustworthy data. But many utilities are sitting on years of poorly documented, inconsistently structured, and incompletely integrated data.

Programs that try to skip the data foundation step in the name of moving fast inevitably pay for it later — with models that can't be trusted, reports that contradict each other, and analysts spending their time cleaning data instead of generating insights.

Investing in data quality, lineage, and governance before building analytics capabilities is slower initially but significantly faster overall.

## 3. Compliance Constraints Are Enablers, Not Obstacles

NERC CIP, FERC Order 2222, and other regulatory requirements are often positioned as constraints that slow transformation. In practice, utilities with strong compliance cultures tend to build more disciplined technology programs.

The discipline required for change management, documentation, and testing in a NERC CIP environment transfers well to modern software delivery. Utilities that embrace this often build more maintainable, auditable systems than less regulated organizations.

## 4. Vendor-Heavy Programs Accumulate Hidden Costs

The appeal of vendor-managed platforms is understandable — they promise to reduce the burden on internal teams. But utilities that have fully outsourced their digital capabilities find themselves dependent on vendor roadmaps, unable to customize for their specific needs, and paying significant per-seat or per-transaction fees at scale.

The most successful programs build internal capability alongside vendor relationships — ensuring the utility can operate, configure, and extend its technology platform independently.

## 5. Operational Buy-In Requires Early Wins

Transformation programs that spend 18 months building platforms before delivering anything to operations teams tend to lose stakeholder support. The teams that will ultimately use new systems need to see early, tangible improvements — and they need to be involved in shaping what gets built.

The programs we've seen succeed consistently find ways to deliver value to operations within the first 90 days, even if those early deliverables are modest. Early wins build credibility and help operations teams become advocates rather than skeptics.

---

Digital transformation in utilities is genuinely hard — the combination of technical complexity, regulatory requirements, and operational criticality makes it unlike most industries. But utilities that approach these programs thoughtfully, with the right mix of internal capability and external expertise, are building real competitive and operational advantages.
